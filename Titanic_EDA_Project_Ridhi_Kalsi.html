🎯 Final Key Takeaways:¶

  • Females had a significantly higher survival rate (74%) compared to males (19%), making gender one of the strongest predictors of survival. This reflects the “women and children first” policy during the evacuation.
  • Passengers in 1st class had the highest survival rate (63%), whereas those in 3rd class had the lowest (24%). This indicates that socioeconomic status and access to lifeboats played a critical role in determining survival.
  • Children (age < 10) had better chances of survival, especially those traveling in 1st or 2nd class. This supports the hypothesis that younger passengers were prioritized during rescue efforts.
  • Embarked location showed some influence, with passengers boarding from Cherbourg (C) having relatively better survival rates than those from Southampton or Queenstown, possibly due to a higher proportion of wealthier passengers boarding there. -Family presence onboard had mixed effects: Passengers with small families (1–2 siblings/spouses or parents/children) had slightly higher survival, suggesting moderate family support might have been helpful during evacuation. -Fare paid was loosely correlated with survival, with higher fares often indicating better class and thus higher chances of rescue.
